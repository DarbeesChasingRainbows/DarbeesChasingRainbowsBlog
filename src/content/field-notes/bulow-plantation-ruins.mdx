## What we noticed

The first thing you notice at Bulow are the **coquina walls** — chunks of fossilized seashell, pressed into stone over thousands of years and quarried by hand. They're the wrong color for Florida — almost honey-pink in late afternoon — and they make you stop talking for a minute.

## Nature observations

We counted at least four species of palm in a single hundred-yard stretch. The cypress along the creek were taller than we expected, with knees rising up out of the duff like little wooden molars. We didn't see alligators today, but the warning signs were not subtle.

## Historical context

Bulowville was once one of the largest sugar and indigo plantations in East Florida. The mill's coquina arches were engineered to vent the heat of the sugar boilers. The site is also a sober place: that wealth was built on the labor of enslaved people, and the ruins speak to both engineering skill and a system that was wrong.

- **History:** Second Seminole War, plantation economy, sugar trade

- **Character:** What does it mean to remember well?

## Would we go again?

Yes. It's quiet, it's free with the state park pass, and it's the kind of place that gives kids real things to ask questions about.
