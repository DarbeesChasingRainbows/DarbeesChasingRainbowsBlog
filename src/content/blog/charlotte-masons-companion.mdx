## Why we picked it back up

We read *A Charlotte Mason Companion* before we ever owned an RV. Five years and twenty-six states later, it reads completely differently — more like a packing list than a philosophy book.

## What still works on the road

**Narration.** Mason's idea that a child telling back what they heard is stronger than any worksheet. It needs no printer, no shelf, no Wi-Fi. We do it after every read-aloud.

**Short lessons.** Twenty minutes per subject sounds restrictive until you try it in a 30-foot rig at a state park with a thunderstorm rolling in. Then it's gospel.

**Living books over textbooks.** A library card and a Kindle replace a whole bookcase.

<Callout type="faith" title="Slow learning, real attention">
Mason's emphasis on attention — really seeing one bird, one painting, one paragraph — fits the rhythm of slow travel better than any curriculum we've tried.
</Callout>

## What quietly doesn't transfer

- Wall timelines and sprawling nature notebooks need square footage we don't have.
- Composer study works in theory, but our truck's road noise wins.
- "Mother culture" — Mason's term for the mom's own reading life — gets crushed unless we actively defend the first 30 minutes of the day.

## How we use it now

We pulled five habits from the book and stapled them to the fridge:

1. Read aloud daily, no matter where we parked.
2. Narrate after every chapter — out loud, no notes.
3. One nature observation a day in the kids' field journals.
4. Short lessons. Stop when you said you'd stop.
5. Mom reads, too. Out loud sometimes, where the kids can see.

## Who this book is for

- Families curious about a gentler, attention-first approach.
- Anyone who's burned out on grade-by-grade curriculum.
- Road-schoolers looking for a flexible spine.

It is *not* a step-by-step curriculum. If that's what you need this season, start somewhere else and come back to it.

## Verdict

Five years later, it still earns its shelf space — which, in an RV, is the highest compliment we know how to give.
