## We did not pick a one-size-fits-all method

When people hear that we homeschool, it is easy to assume we must fit neatly into a named philosophy or educational camp.

We do not.

We have not built our homeschool around one branded method, and we do not think every family has to. That does not mean schooling is casual, optional, or whatever feels easiest that day. Homeschool still has to be school. It has to form a child, train attention, build discipline, teach real skills, and prepare them for the world they are actually going to live in.

For us, that means holding a few convictions tightly and staying flexible about the tools.

## The core comes first

We believe the best way to equip a child in this world is to focus first on the core skills:

- Reading
- Writing
- Math

Those are not flashy. They do not always make for the most exciting story. But they are the foundation everything else leans on.

Reading opens the door to Scripture, history, literature, instructions, contracts, technical documentation, and the ideas of people who are no longer alive to explain themselves.

Writing teaches a child to order their thoughts, communicate clearly, and defend what they believe.

Math trains precision, patience, and problem-solving. Word problems especially matter to us because they force a child to slow down, understand what is being asked, and think critically instead of just repeating a procedure.

## History gives the work roots

Secondarily, we want our children to have a strong understanding of history.

We do not want them floating through the present with no memory of what came before them. History gives context. It shows the consequences of ideas. It gives examples of courage, foolishness, faithfulness, compromise, rebellion, repentance, and endurance.

We often pair more direct historical learning with historical fiction afterward. That kind of story can help a child imagine the people, places, pressures, and ordinary life around the facts they have already studied.

## Story helps difficult subjects stick

We also use story-driven learning in subjects like physics, biology, chemistry, and economics.

Following the arc of a main set of characters can make hard ideas more memorable. A child may not immediately care about an abstract principle, but they may remember what happened to a character who had to test it, use it, or suffer the consequences of misunderstanding it.

That matters because we are not only trying to get through material. We are trying to help our children retain, connect, and apply what they learn.

We cannot separate education from formation. What our children read, practice, remember, imitate,
  and love is shaping the kind of people they are becoming before God.
</Blockquote>

## We review, return, and circle back

We try to borrow from what we learned in _Make It Stick_: learning needs retrieval, review, and repeated contact over time.

In real family life, that means we do not always treat a completed lesson as a finished thing forever. Once a child seems to have mastered something new, we expect to see it again later. We focus on the current grade level while also circling back to the grade behind it.

Sometimes that looks like informal questions after a reading. Sometimes it looks like a child explaining how they solved a problem. Sometimes it looks like realizing that a concept needs another pass before we move forward.

We are not trying to race a calendar. We are trying to make sure the child is prepared for the next set of lessons.

## We use real tools, not a perfect system

Our curriculum and resources are not a single neat stack.

We have used The Good and the Beautiful as a supplement. We use Sonlight and Horizons. We are involved with Classical Conversations during the summer months. We are also evaluating Master Books for next year.

Before this whole season began, one of the books I picked up was _The Well-Trained Mind_. I never finished it, but the appeal of the trivium still makes sense to us: grammar, logic, and rhetoric are a serious way to think about how children grow in knowledge, reasoning, and communication.

The point is not to collect labels. The point is to choose tools that help us teach our children well.

## Nature study is becoming more intentional

We spend time learning about tree, plant, and mushroom identification. We talk about what can be eaten, what cannot, and the rule that you never harvest anything you cannot identify.

We use Peterson Guides and supplemental classes like Silva Tracking. For now, most of this has been observation and identification rather than formal nature notebooks.

Field notes are something we want to grow into. We are not going to pretend it has already been a central habit when it has not. But it fits the kind of learning we value: attention, patience, humility, and respect for God's creation.

## Faith is not an add-on

Our Christian faith shapes what we choose, how we discipline, and how we talk about the world.

We choose Christian-based curricula where possible. We are careful with extracurricular books and media. Some curated lists have served us well, and some have fallen flat.

Discipline is not just behavior management. We want our children to understand why something is wrong, how sin hurts us and others, and why repentance and making amends matter.

History and science conversations come back to the Lord. We talk about the missteps of history, the wonder of creation, and the way men like Sir Isaac Newton saw the study of the created order as connected to the glory of God.

Educational branding cannot be complete or effective without faith and formation.

## RV life changes the school day

Our homeschool does not happen in ideal conditions.

Space is hard. Sometimes we have to take turns at the table because there is not enough room for everyone to work at once.

Storage is hard. Periodically, books have to go into storage because of weight.

Noise is hard. Younger children can be rambunctious while older children are trying to focus and while work still has to get done.

Travel days usually happen on weekends, but not always. When they land during the week, school may need to pause until we get settled again.

Internet matters more than people might think. Starlink has been paramount when we have a clear view of the sky. When we do not, we rely on cellular data, and that can disappear quickly with work demands.

Consistency is still high because my wife carries the homeschool work full-time. But consistency does not mean every day looks identical. In a real family, under real constraints, faithfulness often looks like returning to the work again after the interruption.

## Preparing children for the world they will inherit

We are raising children in a world where AI is now dominant.

That does not make reading, writing, math, history, faith, discernment, and practical skills less important. It makes them more important.

Our children need to know how to think, not just how to search. They need to understand words, numbers, people, incentives, creation, Scripture, and reality. They need to be able to evaluate claims, solve problems, communicate clearly, and tell the difference between a useful tool and a false authority.

That is why we are not chasing a perfect homeschool label.

We are trying to do what is right by our children.

## Where we are still growing

We are still a work in progress.

We want a more intentional evening reading rhythm to help the family wind down before bed. We want to use field notes more deliberately. We are still evaluating curriculum choices. We are still learning what works for each child.

That is part of the point.

You do not have to choose a one-size-fits-all education for your family. But you do have to choose deliberately. You do have to ask what kind of people your children are becoming. You do have to decide what matters most when the table is too small, the internet is unstable, the toddler is loud, and the next lesson still needs to happen.

For us, the answer begins with faith, continues through the core disciplines, and keeps returning to the same question:

Are we preparing our children to live faithfully, think clearly, and glorify God in the world they have actually been given?
