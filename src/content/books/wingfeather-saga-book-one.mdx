## Why we picked it up

We needed a long-running read-aloud series that would survive being interrupted by travel days, dental appointments, and tired parents. Andrew Peterson's name kept coming up in road-school book lists. We started Book One in late winter 2025 and finished it on a slow week in a state park.

## Why it stays on the shelf

Three things earned this book permanent space in a 30-foot rig where every inch is contested:

1. **The Igiby family rhythm** — Janner, Tink, and Leeli feel like our kids' cousins. Their squabbles and loyalties land.
2. **The footnotes** — Peterson treats reading aloud as performance art. The footnotes give the parent reader a moment to breathe and break the fourth wall with the kids.
3. **The pacing** — chapters are exactly the right length for a tired bedtime read, and the cliffhangers do their job without being cheap.

<Callout type="faith" title="Faith inside the story, not on top of it">
  We've shelved Christian fiction that preached at our kids. Peterson does the harder thing — embeds
  a worldview in plot and character so that questions about courage, sacrifice, and providence come
  up naturally. The kids ask the questions. We don't have to manufacture them.
</Callout>

## How we read it

Bedtime, three to four chapters a week. We did NOT split between parents — my wife read the whole series. It works either way, but a single reader's voice gave the kids a steady through-line.

## What we'd do differently

Start sooner. We waited too long thinking the kids weren't ready. They were ready a year before we started.
