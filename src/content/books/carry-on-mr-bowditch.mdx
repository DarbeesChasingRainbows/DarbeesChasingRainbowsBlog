## Why we picked it up

It came up in a Charlotte Mason / classical homeschool book list every time we looked. We had it on the queue for a year before we finally pulled it off the library shelf. We started it on a slow week in middle Tennessee and finished it driving south through Georgia.

## Why it earned a green light

Two reasons:

1. **It's a great story before it's a great unit study.** Bowditch loses parents, mentors, and his first wife. He keeps studying. He reworks an entire navigational reference book that mariners had been quietly dying because of. The plot is the formation.
2. **It's the rare educational book that doesn't telegraph its lessons.** The kids absorbed real history about the American maritime economy, real principles of navigation, and a real model of self-taught expertise — without any of it feeling like a worksheet.

## What it sparked afterward

This is the unusual part. _Carry On, Mr. Bowditch_ didn't end when we finished the book:

- Two weeks of casual conversation about latitude, longitude, and dead reckoning
- A library trip for nautical history books for the older two
- The 10-year-old asking, unprompted, "how do you measure the sun's angle?"
- A homeschool unit study on early American shipping that ran for a month

<Callout type="faith" title="Vocation, modeled">
  Bowditch's life is a model of what the Reformers meant by *vocation* — that a calling is something
  you steward across decades of difficulty. The kids didn't need us to draw the line. They felt it.
</Callout>

## How we read it

Mixed format. Bedtime read-alouds at home; audiobook in the truck on travel days. Splitting like this was easier than we feared — the audiobook narrator was close enough to my wife's read-aloud cadence that the kids didn't mind the switch.

## Who it's for

- Families looking for a high-substance read-aloud
- Homeschoolers who want a book that pulls real curriculum behind it
- Anyone who likes biographies that respect the subject

## What we'd do differently

Start the math/navigation rabbit-hole earlier — by the time we realized the kids wanted more, we were two states away from the maritime museums that would have made it concrete.
